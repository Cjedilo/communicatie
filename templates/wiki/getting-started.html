Getting started

Two paths: join a server someone else runs, or run your own.

As a user As an admin

As a user

Someone shared a link with you

To join a Messages server you need two things: a web address from whoever runs it, and a browser.

Step 1 — Open the address

The server address looks something like https://example.com/chat or https://192.168.1.10:8443. Open it in your browser. You'll see a login page.

Step 2 — Create an account

Click Register, pick a username and a password (at least 8 characters). Your username is your login name — it can't be changed later, so choose something you're happy with.

Your account exists only on this server. There's no central sign-up, no email required.

Step 3 — You're in

After registering you land on the Stream — a feed of recent messages across all channels you have access to. Use the icons on the left to navigate:

🌊Stream — everything at a glance
💬Channels — browse and join conversations
🔗Peers — other servers you're connected to
👥Users — people on this and connected servers
⚙️Server — settings (owner only)
👤Your profile

Step 4 — Make it yours

Go to your profile (👤) to personalise your account:

  • Display name — the name others see in conversations. Your login name stays the same; this is just what's shown.
  • Avatar — upload a photo or image. It appears next to your messages and on your profile. Tap Change avatar to upload one.

Step 5 — Add to your homescreen (optional)

Messages works as a web app on your phone. In your browser, choose Add to homescreen (Chrome: menu → Add to homescreen; Safari: share → Add to homescreen). It opens full-screen, just like a native app.

As an admin

Running your own server

Running a Messages server means you control everything — who can join, what's stored, and how it connects to others.

What you need

  • A machine that stays on — a Raspberry Pi 4 or 5, a mini PC, an old laptop, or a cheap VPS. Something drawing ~5W is plenty for a household or small group.
  • Linux or Windows
  • A stable internet connection — fiber is ideal, but any always-on connection works
  • A domain name or static IP if you want people outside your network to connect

Step 1 — Install

git clone https://github.com/Cjedilo/communicatie.git
cd communicatie
./install.sh        # Windows: install.cmd

The installer asks a few questions — which port to use if 80 or 443 are taken, where to store uploaded images — and sets up Messages as a background service. When it's done, it prints one important URL:

Setup complete. Open this URL to claim your server as admin:

  https://192.168.1.10:8443/setup/a3f9c2...

Keep this URL safe — it's shown once.

Step 2 — Claim your server

Open the URL the installer printed. You'll be asked to create your admin account. The first — and only — person to open that URL becomes the owner with full access to all server settings.

Step 3 — Set your server name and address

Go to ⚙️ Server. Under Identity:

  • Name — how your server appears to peers. Pick something recognisable, like your name or household.
  • Address — the full wss:// address others use to reach you, e.g. wss://myhome.example.com/chat. This is what you share with people you want to connect with.

Step 4 — Open a port

For people outside your home network to reach your server, forward the port you chose during setup on your router to this machine.

Port forwarding varies by router — see the router setup guide for step-by-step instructions for common models.

Step 5 — Invite someone

Copy your server address from the Identity section — it looks like https://myhome.example.com/chat or https://192.168.1.10:8443. Send that to whoever you want to invite. They open it in a browser, register, and they're in.

The wss:// address in the Identity field is for server-to-server federation — that's what you share with other server admins. Regular users always use https://.

A note on the browser warning

If you don't have a domain name and a trusted certificate yet, your browser will show a warning along the lines of "Your connection is not private" the first time someone visits.

This looks worse than it is. The warning doesn't mean the connection is unencrypted — it means the certificate hasn't been signed by a trusted authority. Traffic is still encrypted end-to-end. The warning simply reflects that the browser can't verify the server's identity through a third party.

For a server run by someone you trust — a family member, a friend — this is fine. Click Advanced → Proceed and you're through. Your browser will remember it.

If you want to get rid of the warning entirely, the cleanest path is a real domain name with a Let's Encrypt certificate. Messages can set this up for you automatically — see the SSL certificate guide.

Step 6 — Connect to other servers

Go to 🔗 Peers and paste the address of another Messages server. Once connected, you can see each other's public channels and message each other's users directly.
